AI Agents Explained: Use Cases, Challenges, and Real-World Applications in 2025

Tim Lin
6 min read
·
Apr 1, 2025

--

What Are AI Agents, and How Do They Work?

In recent discussions around large language models (LLMs), there’s been growing interest in using these models not only as conversational partners but also as “core orchestrators” for complex tasks. When viewed in this role, an LLM becomes an Agent — a system capable of goal-driven planning, memory management, and tool usage. Unlike a simple, passive tool that responds to commands, an Agent simulates human-like “agency” by planning, organizing, executing, and learning throughout a task.

Why Agents Represent a Paradigm Shift

Traditional software often operates in a reactive mode, awaiting instructions. Agents, by contrast, can be set within specific boundaries of ability — yet they proactively identify problems, set goals, brainstorm strategies, select solutions, execute tasks, and self-correct. Current AI Agents can be categorized into three main types:

  1. Single Agent: Operates individually to accomplish tasks.
  2. Multi-Agent Collaboration: A group of Agents working together.
  3. Human–Agent Interaction: Agents that cooperate or communicate directly with human users.

From a technical perspective, AI Agents are not a new technology as much as a new managerial framework for LLMs. Instead of a simple “prompt–response” pattern, an Agent provides a dynamic decision-making structure that enables LLMs to handle more complex, real-world tasks.

From Chain of Thought (CoT) to Agent

What Is Chain of Thought (CoT)?

Before diving into how CoT supports Agents, it helps to clarify two concepts: “language intelligence” and “reasoning.” Language intelligence refers to the model’s ability to use natural language for understanding and conceptualizing experiences. Reasoning involves drawing new conclusions from known premises, often requiring multiple steps to reach intermediate concepts.

CoT (Chain of Thought) prompts a large language model to break down a complex problem into smaller, step-by-step subproblems — significantly improving performance and clarity. A typical CoT-style prompt may include:

  1. Instruction: A direct request (e.g., “Solve this complex math problem…”).
  2. Rationale: A series of logical steps or justifications.
  3. Exemplars: Worked examples demonstrating the reasoning process.

Why Use CoT?

  1. Enhanced Reasoning Ability: By decomposing problems, CoT helps models better manage complex tasks and avoid missing critical details.
  2. Improved Explainability: Seeing the step-by-step logic clarifies the model’s internal process, making error analysis easier.
  3. Increased Controllability: Exposing the intermediate steps lets developers guide or correct the reasoning at any point.
  4. Flexibility: A simple prompt like “Let’s think step by step” can enable CoT across various tasks and models.

How CoT Powers Agents

Agents need to deeply understand incoming information, user instructions, and context, then form strategies before taking action. This decision-making process is parallel to how humans solve problems: perceiving the environment, analyzing data, forming a plan, executing actions, observing outcomes, and adjusting. CoT strengthens this workflow by breaking the overall objective into smaller steps, reducing errors, and providing a traceable path for verification. It acts as a reliability engine that helps Agents maintain efficiency in complex real-world scenarios.

Challenges in Deploying Agents

Although AI Agents hold significant promise, real-world adoption faces several obstacles:

  1. Generalization in Unknown Domains: LLMs struggle with tasks that require true interaction with the physical world. An Agent framework that can schedule web browsing may not seamlessly handle physical control tasks like coordinating a fleet of drones.
  2. Excessive Interactions: Agents often engage in multi-step loops that can become repetitive or inefficient, driving up costs in logging, storage, and retrieval.
  3. Personalized Agents: Building a truly personalized Agent for every user is still a work in progress. Different technical paths — prompt customization, fine-tuning, or model editing — each face their own limitations.
  4. Multi-Agent Societies: Scaling up to large communities of Agents highlights issues of computational costs and emergent social behaviors.
  5. Safety Concerns: Privacy, permission abuses, and information toxicity become more pressing as Agents interact extensively with real-world data and tools. Aligning AI systems with human values and avoiding harmful outputs requires robust safeguards.
  6. Agent Evaluation: Traditional benchmarks (like static NLP datasets) do not work well for evaluating Agents that must continuously interact with the environment. Success cannot be judged solely by final output; how well an Agent handles intermediate steps matters, too.

From a practical perspective, two primary barriers often emerge:

  1. API Ecosystems: Rich, high-quality APIs are essential for Agents to perform tasks reliably. Many markets lack the maturity or openness of established ecosystems, limiting real-world adoption.
  2. Open vs. Closed Scenarios: Closed ecosystems (e.g., travel bookings) with comprehensive, stable APIs and finite tasks are easier for Agents to handle. More open-ended domains (e.g., legal assistance) pose additional challenges, as new cases, regulations, and fragmented APIs can lead to confusion and outdated knowledge.

Practical Agent Applications

1. Creative and Generative Assistants

Many enterprise tools integrate LLM-based content generation to automate tasks such as drafting test questions in training modules, producing marketing copy, or generating summarized reports.

  • Single-Agent Content Generation: Uses the model’s generative power to replace or simplify human labor on routine tasks.
  • Multi-Agent Collaboration: For complex projects — like software development — different Agents simulate roles (project manager, developer, QA), working in tandem to produce a finished product.

2. Enterprise Knowledge Assistants

Embedding a private knowledge base to enhance an LLM’s domain-specific information helps reduce hallucinations and provide more accurate answers. This approach is often implemented with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which retrieves relevant texts from a database and feeds them into the model at query time. Frameworks like LangChain or LlamaIndex simplify the process of connecting documents, vector databases, and prompts. Platforms such as Baidu’s Lingjing, COZE, and FastGPT offer out-of-the-box RAG capabilities.

3. Data Analysis Assistants

From everyday spreadsheet queries to advanced BI dashboards, Agents can help translate natural language into data analysis routines via:

  • Text-to-API or Text-to-SQL conversions,
  • Code interpreters that execute Python scripts and generate visualizations or insights.

Projects like DB-GPT, OpenAgents, and OpenInterpreter aim to streamline building these data-driven Agents.

4. Application/Tool Assistants

By mapping natural language to specific API calls, Agents can function as powerful tool orchestrators. Common development stacks include:

  • LangChain Agents, where each “Tool” is abstracted for easy composition.
  • OpenAI Function Calling and Assistants API, enabling more direct integration with GPT-4o.
  • OpenAgents Plugins, a flexible system for adding new plugins and workflows.

5. Custom Workflow Assistants (AI-Driven RPA)

For repetitive tasks or web-based workflows, AI Agents can be combined with RPA-like tools to automate processes such as data collection, merges, calculations, and bulk actions. With advancements in multimodal models (e.g., GPT-4V), Agents may soon autonomously interpret on-screen elements to adapt quickly to changing interfaces.

Where Do Agents Excel in Practice?

Agents often excel in domains where they can surpass the proficiency of an average employee — even if they can’t match top experts. For example, writing SQL queries, generating regular expressions, or drafting routine code can save substantial time and labor. While Agents might produce a “60/100” grade in advanced tasks, it’s usually enough to solve the top issues of the majority of users.

If an Agent can address the most common pain points, users are more inclined to accept occasional inaccuracies in niche scenarios. Over time, user training and feedback loops can further refine the Agent’s performance, creating a symbiotic relationship between humans and AI.

Conclusion

AI Agents extend the potential of large language models far beyond simple Q&A chatbots. By embedding planning, reasoning (through Chain of Thought), tool usage, and dynamic decision-making into AI systems, it becomes possible to delegate increasingly complex tasks to autonomous or semi-autonomous Agents. From generating marketing copy to analyzing corporate data, the scope of these applications is expanding rapidly.

Of course, challenges remain — such as building rich API ecosystems, ensuring safety and alignment, and establishing robust evaluation methods. Yet as the supporting technologies evolve, the future may see AI Agents integrated into everyday workflows, delivering tangible benefits in productivity, insight, and innovation.
